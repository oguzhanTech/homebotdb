**Pemba**, a modified **Unitree G1**, reached the summit of **Chimborazo** in Ecuador after a **16-hour** push to about **20,341 ft (6,200 m)**. That altitude, cold, snow, thin air, and weak comms are a harder test than a trade-show floor. Still, the climb was **not fully autonomous**. On steeper pitches the team **carried** the robot. Treat this as a harsh field trial, not a solo mountaineering win.

## What the team reported

- **Robot:** Pemba, built on a modified **Unitree G1** platform
- **Route:** Chimborazo volcano, Ecuador, one of the highest peaks in the Andes
- **Duration:** about **16 hours** for the summit attempt
- **Independent walking:** on sections where slope stayed **under ~30°**
- **Human assist:** crew carried Pemba on **steeper, technical** passages
- **Stress factors:** cold, snow, **low oxygen**, patchy comms, and **battery load** at altitude

The useful signal is environmental: how joints, balance, and power hold up when the demo hall is gone. The limit is autonomy: when the grade gets real, humans still had to step in.

## Everest next, permits unclear

Reports say **Everest** is the next target. Planned checks include **battery performance**, joint durability, gait on uneven terrain, and behavior in extreme cold between base camps and higher camps.

That plan may stall on paperwork. **Nepal** reportedly has **no clear rules** yet for robotic expeditions on Everest, and officials are said to be drafting guidance for non-human climbing teams. Until permits exist, the Everest leg stays a headline, not a scheduled test.
